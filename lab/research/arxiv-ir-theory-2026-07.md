## bgrep Structural-Leap Research Report

Five parallel research threads, each fetched and cross-checked against primary sources (arXiv abstracts fetched directly for the two highest-stakes claims). All findings below are **deterministic/unsupervised** — no embeddings, no training — as scoped.

---

### 1. Query Performance Prediction (QPP) — target: G2, G4

**Numbers first.** On the 2025 reproducibility study (arXiv:2503.24251), classic unsupervised predictors correlate with true AP only moderately: NQC τ=0.396/ρ=0.332 (TREC Robust), dropping to τ=0.242 on ClueWeb09B — i.e., **QPP signal is real but weak and collection-dependent**. Critically, arXiv:2601.17339 (2026) shows correlation-with-AP is a poor proxy for whether a QPP signal actually helps a pipeline: NQC has the *best* raw correlation (0.386) but a *different* metric (RSD — robust std-dev of scores) delivers the real downstream win (~4.5% AP@100 gain via fusion-gating), and per-query correlation between predicted-quality and actual pipeline benefit is only ρ=0.23–0.57.

- **Leap**: A retrieval system can estimate its own per-query result quality from the score distribution alone (no relevance labels), and this signal is strong enough to gate pipeline decisions — but only if validated on downstream task metrics, not correlation-with-AP alone.
- **Mechanism for bgrep**: Compute NQC/RSD-style features on the raw candidate score list per query (std-dev of top-k scores / mean score) as a **cheap query-difficulty gate**. Route "confident" queries (low score variance, high top-1 gap) through a narrow/fast expansion; route "ambiguous" queries (flat score distribution) through wider per-source caps. Crucially — per finding 2601.17339 — validate the chosen feature against bgrep's own SWE-bench-Lite/archex-bench harness, not against textbook AP correlation.
- **Directly relevant**: arXiv:2304.11752 ("Query-specific Variable Depth Pooling via QPP", SIGIR 2023) already does exactly this for pooling depth — per-query QPP score sets retrieval depth k instead of fixed k. This is a template, not just an analogy, for G4's adaptive-k.

### 2. Optimal stopping / prophet inequalities / Pandora's Box — target: G2, G4

**Numbers first.** TASR (arXiv:2606.13814, KDD 2026 workshop, verified via direct fetch): a **single fixed isotonically-calibrated logit-margin threshold of 0.25** — no classifier, no training — retains **94.8% of fixed-k=5's F1 at 62.6% of its retrieval calls**, and beats fixed-k=3 by **+3.42 F1** at equal call count (55.01 vs 54.33), across 32 configurations with zero regressions. Classic theory backing this shape of rule: the single-threshold prophet inequality guarantees ≥1/2 of offline-optimal value with one static threshold (Krengel-Sucheston/Samuel-Cahn), and a single quantile-probe extension pushes this to 0.6718 (arXiv:2205.05519) vs the 1-1/e≈0.632 no-probe bound.

- **Leap**: The clean alternative to a fixed-k or fixed-count cutoff is not a smarter count — it's a **calibrated stop-index computed once per candidate/round** (Pandora's Box: open in decreasing-index order, stop when best-seen exceeds every unopened index), which requires zero re-optimization at query time and carries a formal ≥1/2-of-optimal guarantee.
- **Mechanism for bgrep**: Replace G2's fixed per-source cap + score-ratio threshold with a **Weitzman-style index per candidate**: precompute (offline, deterministically) a reservation value per candidate source combining its historical hit-rate and its "cost" (tokens to inspect). At query time, expand candidates in index order across *all* sources jointly (not per-source count), and stop the whole pool — not each source independently — once the best-found score exceeds the index of every remaining unopened candidate. This directly kills the JS/TS starvation failure mode: a good candidate in a capped source is never blocked by an arbitrary per-source count, only by a globally-comparable index.
- Also directly on point: arXiv:2602.03720 ("Nested Search," Feb 2026) extends the Pandora's Box index policy to hierarchical/branching search — a close formal analogue to bgrep's coarse-pool → rerank → truncate cascade, worth a deeper read if G3's clustering work (below) gets implemented.

### 3. Rank-fusion / cascade theory — target: G1

**Numbers first.** Certified Error Control of Candidate Set Pruning (arXiv:2205.09638, SIGIR 2022) uses split-conformal calibration to prune a stage-1 candidate set to **27/1000 documents (~37x reduction)** while holding final MRR@10 ≥0.38 with ~90% empirical coverage — a genuine statistical guarantee, not a heuristic threshold. Contrast: naive fusion is not safe — Benham & Culpepper (ADCS 2017) show RRF still produces **21 per-topic losses vs. the best single system** in their test set, i.e., no fusion method tested is risk-free. Fox & Shaw / Lee's classic result: fusion gains are largest when component lists are comparably effective but low-overlap; correlated/similar lists barely help.

- **Leap**: There is no monotonicity guarantee for head-of-list fusion — "fuse everything" can *hurt* P@1 — but conformal-prediction-style calibration converts an arbitrary pruning threshold into one with a provable coverage bound, and risk-sensitive fusion diagnostics (URisk) can tell you *in advance* whether fusing two signal sources is likely to help or hurt for a given query.
- **Mechanism for bgrep**: Before blindly fusing lexical signal + any secondary signal (symbol-graph proximity, path proximity, etc.) at the head of the ranked list, compute an overlap/agreement statistic between the two ranked lists for *this query* (Jaccard of top-k, or Kendall-tau). Only fuse/reorder the head when the lists are low-overlap-but-comparable-quality (the Fox&Shaw/Lee condition); when the lists agree already, skip fusion (there's nothing to gain and a real chance to hurt File@1). For a harder guarantee, calibrate a conformal threshold offline on held-out queries so any head-reordering has a certified worst-case bound on how much it can drop true-positive rank — turning G1's reordering into a bounded-risk operation instead of a blind heuristic.

### 4. Submodular/matroid group-budget theory — target: G2

**Numbers first.** Per-source caps are literally a **partition matroid constraint** (one part per source, capacity = cap). Classic result: greedy under a general matroid constraint only guarantees **1/2**-approximation (Fisher-Nemhauser-Wolsey 1978) vs. **1-1/e≈0.632** for a simple cardinality budget — matroid structure costs you approximation quality unless you do continuous-greedy. The directly relevant 2026 result: arXiv:2601.09860 ("Improved Algorithms for Fair Matroid Submodular Maximization," Microsoft Research) shows prior fairness-constrained algorithms only guaranteed **⌊ℓ/2⌋** of the required per-group minimum (useless at ℓ=1, i.e., exactly bgrep's "at least one file from this source" case) — their new algorithm achieves a constant-factor objective approximation while losing only a **(1-ε)** multiplicative factor on the per-group floor, i.e., near-full fairness instead of 50%.

- **Leap**: Fixed per-source caps are a crude partition matroid; the fix is not a bigger cap, it's **redistributing unused capacity from underfilled groups to overfilled groups while keeping a near-full per-group floor** — a formally studied (as of 2026) constant-factor-approximable problem, not an ad hoc heuristic.
- **Mechanism for bgrep**: Implement a two-pass cap allocation: pass 1, run each source's expansion against a *soft* floor (guarantee ≥1 or ≥2 candidates survive per source, matching the (1-ε) fairness floor result); pass 2, pool all remaining budget and allocate by marginal-gain-per-token across sources (submodular greedy), not by re-imposing the original fixed cap. This is exactly what "adaptive per-source caps that reallocate slack" should look like, and it has an approximation-guarantee lineage to point to instead of being an unprincipled patch. Note: the search explicitly found no paper doing this for retrieval/code-search specifically — this is a genuine transfer, not a reproduction.

### 5. Cluster hypothesis / hierarchical retrieval — target: G3

**Numbers first.** LARGER (arXiv:2605.16352, 2026, verified via direct fetch): lexical-match-anchored graph traversal (imports/call-chains/type hierarchies, no embeddings) gets **+13.9 points file-level Acc@5 on LocBench** with tuned hyperparameters, **+11.8 points with fixed hyperparameters**, over the strongest baseline, with consistent gains on 3 more benchmarks. GraphCoder (arXiv:2406.07003, ASE 2024, deterministic BM25/Jaccard coarse stage + code-context-graph fine stage): **+6.06 EM (code match)**, retrieval **1.08s vs 14.02s (~13x faster)**, index **79.5% fewer entries** than RepoCoder-style baselines.

- **Leap**: The cluster hypothesis's modern, empirically strong incarnation for code is *not* embedding-space clustering — it's **lexical-anchor-then-structural-graph-expansion** (import/call/type edges), which gets double-digit file-localization gains while staying fully deterministic and 13x faster than dense/graph-heavy baselines.
- **Mechanism for bgrep for G3 specifically**: In a 12k-file monorepo, don't run lexical scoring flat across all files. First use a handful of highest-IDF query terms to find a small seed set of exact/near lexical hits (even weak ones survive at seed stage). Then expand outward one hop at a time along the *structural* graph (imports, symbol-definition/reference edges, directory adjacency) — exactly LARGER's confidence-filtered local expansion — before running the full lexical scoring pass only within the reached region. This solves dilution directly: instead of asking "which of 12k files scores highest" (where everything is diluted to noise), ask "which small structural neighborhood does the seed lexical hit anchor to," then rank only inside that neighborhood. The search found no published Louvain/Leiden-on-import-graph precedent with numbers — that remains open, but the anchor-then-expand pattern (LARGER, GraphCoder) is directly validated at the scale bgrep needs.

| Rank | Finding | Gap(s) | Why top-ranked |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Lexical-anchor + structural-graph expansion** (LARGER +13.9 Acc@5, GraphCoder 13x speedup, both deterministic) | **G3** (primary), G1 (secondary — right region → right file) | Largest, best-verified numeric win found in the entire search; bgrep already has an import/symbol graph in its expansion stage per its own architecture — this is "point the existing graph the other direction" rather than new infrastructure. Directly fixes the diagnosed root cause (dilution) rather than a downstream symptom. |
| **2** | **Calibrated single-threshold stopping (TASR pattern / prophet-inequality threshold)** — 94.8% quality at 62.6% cost, +1/2-optimal guarantee | **G2** (primary), **G4** | Cheapest possible implementation (one calibrated scalar threshold, no training, computed once offline) for the largest measured failure mode (JS/TS cap starvation). Directly replaces "fixed count + score-ratio" with a principled index-based stop rule; theory gives a floor guarantee, not just an empirical hope. |
| **3** | **Adaptive partition-matroid budget reallocation** (near-full-fairness matroid submodular result, arXiv:2601.09860) | **G2** | Same failure mode as #2 but attacks it structurally (redistribute unused per-source capacity) rather than just changing when to stop. Slightly higher implementation cost (needs a two-pass allocation, not one threshold) — hence ranked below #2, but the two combine cleanly: threshold decides *when* to stop per source, matroid reallocation decides *how much budget* each source starts with. |
| **4** | **NQC/RSD-style score-distribution gating for adaptive-k** (QPP-for-pooling-depth pattern, arXiv:2304.11752) | **G4** (primary), G2 | Real but weaker/noisier signal (τ~0.2–0.4, and correlation≠downstream-utility per 2601.17339) — needs validation against bgrep's own bench before trusting it, which is extra cost. Still cheap to compute (pure score statistics) and composes with #2/#3 as the difficulty-routing layer. |
| **5** | **Risk-gated head reordering** (overlap-conditioned fusion + conformal-calibrated pruning bound) | **G1** | Real fix for the head-precision problem, but the theory here is the least "plug and play": needs an offline calibration set to get the conformal guarantee, and the overlap-gating heuristic (Fox&Shaw/Lee condition) needs tuning per signal pair. Highest payoff ceiling (File@1 is bgrep's worst-measured metric) but highest implementation and validation cost of the five — hence last. |

**One overall note on numbers vs. hype**: two of the five families (QPP, rank fusion) come with an explicit 2025/2026 warning from their own literature — QPP correlation-with-AP is a weak proxy for real utility (ρ=0.23–0.57 at best), and fusion is measurably *not* risk-free (21/N per-topic losses in the cited study). Both should be treated as candidate signals to validate against bgrep's existing SWE-bench-Lite/archex harness, not as drop-in fixes — this matches the project's own house style (numbers-first, measured, no unverified adoption) per the retrieval-recipe-findings memory.
