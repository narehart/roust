## roust Region-Packing Objective Research Report

Six research threads on span/passage-selection theory, each checked against fetched arXiv abstracts/HTML (not just search snippets) where the numbers matter. Scoped to the measured failure: **greedy budgeted-maximum-coverage is optimizing the wrong function** — term-coverage density selects term-dense-but-wrong spans, never declines to return less than 8192 tokens, and the result is 1,100 lines/instance at 0.45% precision with only 45.6% gold-line capture (ContextBench: 0.274 vs 0.374 best-agent). This report only covers objective/selection-scheme literature; it does not re-cover QPP, Pandora stopping, rank fusion, matroid caps, or cluster-hypothesis material from `arxiv-ir-theory-2026-07.md`.

---

### 1. Submodular objective composition beyond term-coverage — the Lin–Bilmes lineage, applied to budgeted RAG context

**Numbers first.** "What Survives Into Context" (arXiv:2607.00725, verified via direct HTML fetch) runs the *exact* algorithmic family roust already uses — greedy, marginal-gain-per-token, knapsack-constrained — but swaps the objective from single-term coverage to a four-term composite: **F(S) = w_rel·Rel(S) + w_qry·QueryCov(S) + w_cov·Repr(S) + w_div·Div(S)**, where Repr(S) is explicitly a "saturated facility-location term" (Lin & Bilmes 2011, ACL — `C_i(S) = min{C_i(S), α·C_i(V)}`, the classic diminishing-returns-on-redundant-coverage device) and Div(S) is a concave-over-sources spread term. On HotpotQA at a 160-token budget (500 questions, 3-seed pooled): submodular packing gets **F1 0.451 / EM 0.359 at 145.5 tokens**, beating naive coverage-only packing (0.400/0.306, +0.051 F1, bootstrap CI [+0.030,+0.072]) and beating an MMR-style pairwise-redundancy packer (0.410/0.318, +0.022 F1 over the runner-up). Their own diagnostic metric ("answer-in-context") correlates with F1 at r=0.50 vs r=0.31 for plain recall@5 — i.e., **recall is not the bottleneck metric once you're past ~90% file recall**, precision-style presence-of-answer-content is. Critically for the "budget always filled" symptom: their budget sweep shows an inverted-U — the greedy procedure does **not** force exhaustion of the 160-token cap when marginal terms go negative, it stops naturally at ~145/160.

- **Mechanism for roust**: the current gain function is `weighted_coverage(NEW query terms) × file_relevance / token_cost` — literally just the QueryCov(S) term above, missing Repr(S) (does this span look like a *representative*/definitional occurrence vs. an incidental keyword mention?) and Div(S) (stop reselecting near-identical regions from the same file once one representative block is in). Adding a saturated-coverage term over an existing similarity signal (line-to-line token-Jaccard, already computable with zero new dependencies) directly targets "wrong lines within right file": a span that merely repeats query terms already covered by a selected span gets diminishing marginal gain, freeing budget for spans that cover *different* gold-adjacent content.
- **Transferability verdict**: **direct, low-effort transplant.** Same optimizer family, same knapsack constraint, deterministic, no embeddings required if similarity is computed lexically. This is an objective-function edit inside roust's existing greedy loop, not new infrastructure. Caveat: paper is a July 2026 preprint (author: Ananto Nayan Bala, single-author, not yet peer-reviewed) — numbers are internally consistent and mechanism is a straightforward classic-theory application, but treat as unreplicated pending independent confirmation.

### 2. Precision-aware / adaptive stopping — the direct fix for "always fills 8192 tokens"

**Numbers first.** Adaptive-k (arXiv:2506.08479, **EMNLP 2025 main**, Taguchi/Maekawa/Bhutani, verified via direct fetch) is fully training-free and deterministic: it looks at the **distribution of query–candidate similarity scores** among already-computed candidates and cuts the selection where the score distribution indicates diminishing relevance, rather than filling a fixed k or fixed token count. Result: **up to 10x fewer tokens than full-context input while still retrieving ~70% of relevant passages**, and it matches-or-beats fixed-k baselines on both factoid and aggregation QA across five long-context LMs and two embedding models — critically, it beats iterative alternatives (Self-RAG, Self-Route) specifically on aggregation-style queries where "the optimal context size is both unknown and variable," which is roust's exact situation (some queries genuinely need 2 lines, others need 40). A second, complementary training-free result — TARG (arXiv:2511.09803, "Retrieval as a Decision") — uses a no-context draft-generation uncertainty score (entropy or logit-margin) as a threshold gate on *whether to retrieve further at all*, cutting retrieval calls 70-90% with no F1 loss vs. always-retrieving; conceptually a decision-layer analog one level up from Adaptive-k, less directly applicable to span packing but same "score-distribution → stop, don't exhaust" pattern.

- **Mechanism for roust**: replace "pack until 8192 tokens or no candidates left" with a **single calibrated threshold on the marginal-gain-per-token curve already produced by the greedy loop**: once marginal gain drops below (e.g.) a fixed fraction of the running mean/first-selected gain, stop — even at 1,000 tokens if that's where genuine query-term novelty runs out. This is the single cheapest, most surgical fix for the reported symptom (1,100 lines returned unconditionally): it doesn't change *what* gets ranked, only *when packing halts*, so it composes cleanly with fix #1 (better objective) and fix #3 (redundancy penalty) rather than competing with them.
- **Transferability verdict**: **direct, lowest-effort transplant of the three.** No new similarity computation needed — it operates purely on the marginal-gain sequence roust's greedy loop already emits internally. Purely a stopping-rule change.

### 3. Redundancy-aware / MMR-under-budget objectives with closed-form (no-tuning) tradeoff calibration

**Numbers first.** Two 2025-2026 papers instantiate the classic Carbonell & Goldstein (SIGIR 1998) MMR relevance-redundancy tradeoff as a deterministic, budget-constrained greedy selector with **no manually-tuned λ**: AdaGReS (arXiv:2512.25052, verified via fetch) derives a closed-form calibration `β* = (α·E[sim(q,x)]) / ((k̄−1)/2·E[sim(x,y)])` from the token budget and average chunk length, eliminating the usual hand-tuned relevance/redundancy knob — but the paper's own released numbers are weak (only IOU-via-figure and one qualitative example, no F1/EM table, so treat as directional not load-bearing). PACMS (arXiv:2606.20047, verified via fetch) instead sums coverage-weighted-by-relevance over the *whole* candidate pool rather than penalizing pairwise similarity among *selected* items (a facility-location variant of the same idea) and reports it beating an MMR baseline head-to-head on end-to-end QA accuracy at matched 45% budget (52.0%/68.0% for two readers vs. 44.0%/56.0% for MMR) despite *matching* MMR's recall — i.e., MMR-style redundancy suppression alone doesn't fix precision, facility-location-style coverage-with-relevance-weighting does, which corroborates finding #1's Repr(S) design over a pure pairwise-diversity term.

- **Mechanism for roust**: the 1,100-line/0.45%-precision symptom is consistent with the greedy loop re-selecting near-duplicate regions (repeated boilerplate around each keyword hit, or multiple overlapping windows of the same function) once "coverage of NEW query terms" runs dry within a file but budget remains — because nothing in the current objective explicitly penalizes selecting spans similar to what's already in the bundle. A lexical-Jaccard-based redundancy discount on marginal gain (deterministic, no embeddings) is a 1-line addition to the existing gain formula.
- **Transferability verdict**: **transplant, medium effort** — needs a candidate-pair similarity function (cheap: token-set Jaccard between newly-scored span and all currently-selected spans, recomputed each greedy step, same asymptotic cost class as the existing per-step scoring). Ranked below #1/#2 because its *numeric* backing is the weakest of the three (AdaGReS has no real eval table; PACMS's numbers are for LLM-agent memory pruning, a different task shape) — it's the right *idea* with the least-verified *evidence*, whereas #1 already packages this exact idea (as Div(S)) inside a result set with real F1 deltas.

### 4. Graph-centrality / LexRank-descendant query-focused extraction

**Numbers first.** LexRank itself (Erkan & Radev, JAIR 2004) and its query-focused descendants are largely **superseded by hybrid lexical+structural graphs** in the training-free-compression literature as of 2026: "From Similarity to Structure" (arXiv:2604.23277, verified via fetch) builds a sparse hybrid sentence graph (mutual-kNN semantic edges + short-range sequential edges), extracts a topic skeleton via clustering, and scores candidates on task-relevance + cluster-representativeness + **bridge centrality** + cycle-coverage, then does budgeted greedy selection with redundancy suppression — training-free and reports being "competitive with strong extractive/abstractive baselines" with larger gains on long documents, though the fetched abstract did not surface a hard numeric table (would need the PDF body for exact deltas). The centrality signal here (bridge centrality: a sentence that connects otherwise-disconnected topic clusters) is a genuinely new idea beyond raw eigenvector centrality — it rewards *connective* spans, which for code could map to spans that bridge a query-matched symbol to its usage site.

- **Mechanism for roust**: the semantic (kNN) edges require an embedding model, which breaks the deterministic/no-model constraint; but the **sequential edges + bridge-centrality scoring** could be reconstructed purely lexically (edges = shared identifier/import references between candidate lines, exactly the kind of structural graph roust's own import/symbol expansion already builds per the retrieval-recipe memory) — i.e., a lexical-only partial reproduction is plausible but is a new-graph-construction task, not a drop-in objective swap.
- **Transferability verdict**: **conceptually relevant, high effort, partial transferability** — the semantic-edge half needs embeddings (drop it); the bridge-centrality idea over a lexical graph is a genuine research transfer roust would have to build and validate itself, not adopt wholesale. Rank accordingly below #1-3.

### 5. Trained context-pruning precedents — what they keep/drop, and why they don't transplant directly

**Numbers first.** RECOMP (arXiv:2310.04408, Xu/Shi/Choi) trains an extractive sentence-selector (dual-encoder, contrastive) that compresses to as low as 6% of original length with minimal QA-performance loss, and — notably — can **emit an empty string** when nothing retrieved is useful ("selective augmentation"). Provence (arXiv:2501.16214, ICLR 2025, verified via direct fetch of full numeric tables) reframes pruning as **token-level sequence labeling** with a single **global, uncalibrated-per-query threshold T** (T=0.1 default): at T=0.1 it compresses 49-69% across seven QA datasets while matching-or-beating full-context LLM-eval scores (e.g. NQ: 72.4 vs 71.8 full-context baseline), and at T=0.5 compression rises to 76-85% with comparable quality; the amount kept is genuinely per-query variable — full contexts are kept when everything scores above threshold, contexts are pruned to zero sentences on "no answer" cases. Both substantially outperform LLMLingua2/LongLLMLingua at matched compression.

- **Mechanism for roust**: neither approach is training-free (RECOMP needs a trained dual-encoder; Provence needs a trained DeBERTa-style token-probability model), so neither transplants as-is. But the *design pattern* is exactly what #2 above proposes in deterministic form: **one fixed, globally-calibrated threshold applied to an already-computed per-unit relevance score, producing genuinely variable output length** — Provence's own ablation shows a single T=0.1 works robustly across seven datasets without per-query retuning, which is direct empirical support that a fixed threshold (not a re-optimized one) is sufficient, strengthening the case for #2's approach over anything requiring online recalibration.
- **Transferability verdict**: **not directly transplantable** (both require training), but they are the strongest available evidence that *the single-fixed-threshold idea generalizes across datasets without per-query tuning* — cited here to de-risk #2, not as a standalone candidate.

### 6. Passage/span-utility estimation without labels — pseudo-relevance feedback and query likelihood at sub-document granularity

**Numbers first.** This thread came back weak relative to the others. Query-likelihood/PRF literature found (arXiv:2510.25488 "Generalized Pseudo-Relevance Feedback," arXiv:2603.11008 "A Systematic Study of PRF with LLMs," arXiv:2205.05888 on feedback-signal quality) all operate at **document granularity** — using top-k retrieved *documents'* terms to expand the query (RM3-style, already covered at doc-level in bgrep's own retrieval-recipe memory) — none found do this at the **span/line granularity** roust needs (i.e., using already-selected high-scoring lines within a file as a local pseudo-relevance seed to re-rank *other* candidate lines in the same file). The one training-free unsupervised passage-scoring method surfaced (UPR — log-probability of generating the query from a candidate passage under a language model) is unsupervised in the sense of needing no labels, but is **not deterministic/model-free**: it requires running an LM's conditional log-likelihood, which is exactly the "no model" constraint roust is trying to stay under.

- **Mechanism for roust (proposed, not found in literature)**: the closest defensible deterministic construction is a **within-file RM3 analog at line-granularity** — take the terms distinctive to the already-selected top-scoring lines in a file (already computed as part of file-level BM25/RM3 per the existing pipeline) and use them to re-score *candidate* lines in the same file that lack direct query-term overlap but share distinctive vocabulary with the seed lines. This is a genuine transfer (extending the memory's already-shipped doc-level RM3 mechanism down one granularity level), not a reproduction of a published result — flagged accordingly, same as the arxiv-ir-theory report flagged the matroid-reallocation and Louvain-on-import-graph gaps.
- **Transferability verdict**: **no strong deterministic precedent exists at this granularity** — this is real, unclaimed territory rather than a transplant; lowest confidence of the six threads, included for completeness per the question's explicit ask rather than as a ranked recommendation.

**Also considered, not ranked top-3**: GeoRAG facility-location-via-Sinkhorn (arXiv:2606.29328, +6.5-9.7 EM vs top-k truncation) — strong numbers but needs LLM-generated sub-queries to build its demand distribution, breaking the training-free constraint; SMART-RAG DPP selection (arXiv:2409.13992) and the hybrid-graph bridge-centrality approach (arXiv:2604.23277) — both training-free in spirit but need an embedding-based similarity kernel for their strongest edges, so only partially transplantable without substituting a lexical kernel (untested substitution, own validation required); Provence (arXiv:2501.16214, ICLR 2025) and RECOMP (arXiv:2310.04408) — best empirical evidence that a single fixed threshold generalizes across datasets without per-query tuning (de-risks #2), but both require training a scorer, so not directly transplantable; ContextSniper (arXiv:2607.01916) — 38-51% token reduction with unchanged SWE-bench-Lite resolution rate in an adjacent code-agent setting, but it prunes agent-loop tool-output at a different granularity via a trained hybrid ranker + intention gate, not deterministic one-shot region packing; TARG (arXiv:2511.09803) and Stop-RAG (arXiv:2510.14337) — training-free retrieval-decision gating (whether/how much to retrieve at all), a layer above span packing, same "score-distribution → stop" pattern as #2 but wrong granularity for this exact fix.

**One gap explicitly flagged, not filled**: span/line-granularity pseudo-relevance feedback (within-file RM3 analog) has no published deterministic precedent found in this search — it is a genuine, unclaimed transfer from bgrep's own shipped doc-level RM3 mechanism, not a literature reproduction, and should be treated as exploratory rather than evidence-backed if attempted.
