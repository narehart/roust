# Statement/Method-Level Fault & Edit Localization Without Tests or Training — Literature Scan

Scope: roust already nails file-level localization (92.3% all-gold, SWE-bench Lite) but caps at 45.6% gold-line recall once inside the right file, because its region selector optimizes query-term density — a signal that correlates weakly with where edits actually land, and a symbol-name bonus already measured net-harmful. This scan hunts specifically for **non-textual, execution-free, training-free** signals that predict *where in a file* an edit lands: change-history/recency priors, AST/structural salience, and query-structure-aware IR — deliberately skipping ground already covered in `wave1/fault-localization.md` (call-graph PageRank, rumor centrality, SBEST stack-trace reachability, SBFL/MBFL ceilings, DepGraph/GraMuS GNNs) and `arxiv-se-networks-2026-07.md` (Leiden clustering, heat-kernel/resistance centrality, Jarvis dispatch resolution, ISSTA'26 anchoring).

## Ranked top 3 transplants for roust's region/line-level failure mode

### #1 — GLANCE family: syntactic/structural salience as an untrained per-line score → target: **within-file line ranking, orthogonal to term density**

Guo et al., **"Code-line-level Bugginess Identification: How Far Have We Come, and How Far Have We Yet to Go?"** (ACM TOSEM 32(4), 2023, DOI:10.1145/3582572) introduce **GLANCE-LR**, a fully deterministic, unsupervised two-stage scorer: after a lightweight file filter, every candidate line gets `DefectPronenessScore = NumTokens · (NumFunctionCalls + 1)`, with any line containing a control keyword (`if/for/while/switch/return`) given an unconditional rank boost. No training, no execution — just a tokenizer/parser pass, which roust already has via tree-sitter. On the 19-project/142-release **BugDet** benchmark it is competitive with or beats deep-learning line-level predictors (DeepLineDP): baseline numbers reported are Mean Recall 0.707, FAR 0.182, MCC 0.141, Median IFA 36, Recall@20%LOC 0.625, HoB 83%.

Mujaddid & Utami's 2025 follow-up, **"Enhancing the GLANCE Framework for Line-Level Defect Prediction"** (Telematika 18(2), Aug 2025, DOI:10.35671/telematika.v18i2.3196), replaces the raw token/call-count score with three still-fully-deterministic semantic features — Cognitive Complexity Score (nesting depth + control-flow-break keywords, à la SonarSource cognitive complexity), API-Weighted function-call count (extra weight for `java.io/nio/net/sql/rmi/reflect/thread/concurrent`-type calls), and Variable-Write Count (assignment/compound-assignment/increment ops per line) — combined as a weighted linear sum. Measured on the same 19-project benchmark (123 cross-release pairs, Wilcoxon signed-rank + BH-correction + Cliff's δ): this variant delivers a statistically significant (p<0.001, small-to-medium effect) drop in False Alarm Rate (0.182→0.146-0.149 median) and rise in MCC (0.141→0.157-0.159 median), at the cost of a medium-effect (δ≈-0.35) Recall drop (0.783→0.711-0.718 median) — i.e., it becomes a stricter, higher-precision ranker that misses simple low-complexity bugs. Runtime is negligible (0.0044 s/KLOC, ~230k LOC/sec on a laptop CPU, no GPU).

**Transferability verdict**: fully deterministic, zero training, zero execution — the single cleanest match to the ask ("AST/structural salience... complexity, fan-in"). **Transplant sketch for roust**: at region-scoring time, compute a per-line/per-span structural-salience score (token count × call count, or the cognitive-complexity variant) from the tree-sitter parse roust already builds, and blend it additively with the existing term-density score rather than replacing it — this avoids the failure mode measured with the symbol-name bonus (which apparently dominated/saturated the objective instead of complementing it). Effort: **low** — no new infrastructure, pure feature engineering on data roust already has in memory.

### #2 — Linespots: exponential-recency-weighted per-line historical-fault density → target: **within-file line ranking from git history, independent of query text**

Scholz & Torkar, **"An Empirical Study of Linespots: A Novel Past-Fault Algorithm"** (arXiv:2007.09394, *Software Testing, Verification and Reliability*, 2021) generalize Google's Bugspots from file- to **line-granularity**: walk commit history, and for every line touched by a fix-inducing commit add `1/(1+exp(-12·age(commit)+12))` to that line's score (age measured in commit-index units, decaying older fixes toward zero over ~500 commits); unmodified lines adjacent to a fix-inducing hunk inherit the hunk's mean score. This is a closed-form recursion over `git log`/diff hunks — no ML, no execution, fully reimplementable from the pseudocode in the paper. Measured on 32 real projects (angular, cpython, scikit-learn, ffmpeg, etc.), against file-level Bugspots mapped down to lines: **EXAM score 0.209 vs 0.225** (lines needed to inspect to hit the first fault, as a fraction of file size), **E_inspect_F 310 vs 1318 lines** (Linespots needs 24% of the lines Bugspots needs to find the first true fault, on average), **AUCEC5 0.231 vs 0.159**, **AUCEC1 0.102 vs 0.063** — consistent gains across all 7 evaluation metrics, at a runtime cost of ~11s vs ~0.01s per sample (still trivially cacheable at index time, matching roust's existing 228ms→9ms cache pattern).

**Transferability verdict**: fully deterministic, execution-free, and it directly targets both the "change-history line-level priors" and "recency prior" items in the question — arguably the most literal match found. Flagged: paper is 2020/2021 (pre-2023 window), included because it is the most rigorously-measured, directly-reimplementable instance of this idea; I found no 2023-2026 successor that improves on it (only industrial replications of *coarser*, trained, file/component-level composites — see also-considered). One methodological trap worth inheriting the warning from: a 2026 replication of a related approach, **"An Extensive Replication Study of the ABLoTS Approach for Bug Localization"** (arXiv:2605.11790), found the original paper's history-recency component leaked future information by keying on bug-*fix* date instead of bug-*report*/query date — any Linespots-style recency prior in roust must decay from the query time, not from a future fix time, or it silently cheats.

**Transplant sketch for roust**: at index time (or lazily, cached per-file), run one pass of `git log -p`/hunk tracking per file to build the same exponential-decay per-line score from fix-labeled commits (or, if roust has no fix-commit labels, from raw churn recency as a weaker proxy — Bugspots-without-fix-detection still beats no-history baselines per D'Ambros et al., cited in the paper's related work). Blend as a third additive score alongside term density and structural salience (#1). Effort: **medium** — needs a git-history mining pass and cache, more infrastructure than #1 but still self-contained and offline.

### #3 — Method-level change-proneness as a weak, secondary tie-breaking prior (with an explicit negative-result warning) → target: **method-granularity fallback signal**

Chowdhury et al., **"Method-Level Bug Prediction: Problems and Promises"** (ACM TOSEM, 2024, DOI:10.1145/3640331) measure four change-history indicators over a method's first five years — revision count, diff size, added-lines-only, Levenshtein edit distance — and find buggy methods have **statistically significantly larger values on all four, with large effect sizes**, replicated across projects. But when the same signals are formalized into a trained classifier, **precision and F1 stay poor (F1 within ~0.25) in cross-project settings**, improving only when both cross-project pooling *and* past project-specific data are combined. The follow-up **"Revisiting Method-Level Change Prediction: A Comparative Evaluation at Different Granularities"** (arXiv:2502.17908, 2025) confirms method-level outperforms class-level when both are scored at method granularity (median accuracy delta 0.26), reinforcing method as the right unit but not resolving the precision problem. A more extreme companion study, **"The Repeat Offenders: Characterizing and Predicting Extremely Bug-Prone Source Methods"** (arXiv:2511.22726, 2025), finds "extremely buggy" methods (>1 bug fix) are only 0.04-6.63% of methods per project, and that even ML classifiers over 14 inception-time structural metrics (SLOC, McCabe complexity, nesting depth, fan-out, maintainability index) get **precision <0.50 in 95% of projects** — a direct, recent confirmation of the older **"On the Performance of Method-Level Bug Prediction: A Negative Result"** (Pascarella et al., *J. Systems and Software* 2019) finding that release-by-release evaluation collapses these models to near-random.

**Transferability verdict**: the raw signal (methods with more historical churn are more likely targets) is real and deterministic (a git-log commit-count per method span, no training needed to *compute* it) — but every attempt to turn it into a confident standalone discriminator has failed, repeatedly, across five years and multiple re-evaluations. **Transplant sketch**: use as a **tertiary tie-breaker only** — e.g., among regions/lines tied on term-density + structural-salience + recency (#1/#2), prefer the one in a method with a higher lifetime revision count — never as a primary ranking signal, and expect it to move the needle only marginally given the field's own repeated negative results at this exact granularity. Effort: **low** (one `git log --follow` pass per method span) but expected-value low too; include mainly because it's the most direct match to "hot methods" and worth having as a cheap nudge once #1/#2 are in place, not before.

## Other findings, by topic (not top-3 but relevant)

**Query-structure-aware IR (stack traces / identifiers vs. prose).** IQLoc (2025, combines IR + fine-tuned transformer for query reformulation) reports differentiated gains by bug-report type — **+118.7% MAP for stack-trace reports, +111.9% for reports with code elements, +127.5% for prose-only reports** vs. a plain-IR baseline — but requires a trained transformer, so it's a ceiling reference, not a transplant. Its design lesson is still usable deterministically: treat stack-trace/quoted-identifier queries and prose queries as different regimes with different scoring, rather than one global recipe — consistent with BLIZZARD's older (2018) finding, restated in follow-ups, that *excessive* structured info in a query can hurt naive lexical scoring as much as sparse info. "Improving IR-based Bug Localization with Semantics-Driven Query Reduction" (arXiv:2510.04468, 2025) pursues the same idea but again leans on a trained semantic-reduction component; I could not extract deterministic-only ablation numbers from the abstract.

**Industrial file/component-level grounding (why line/method granularity is the field's real frontier).** "Bug-Report-Driven Fault Localization: Industrial Benchmarking and Lessons Learned at ABB Robotics" (arXiv:2604.25700, EASE 2026) — a fully non-neural, text-only production study (660 bug reports, 5 years, ABB Robotics) — found simple **TF-IDF + Logistic Regression/SVM beats fine-tuned RoBERTa/DistilRoBERTa** for component-level (subfolder) localization (Top-1≈0.53, Recall@5≈0.79, MAP≈0.61), and cites related work at the same coarse granularity: Chen et al.'s co-fix PageRank (Top-10 32-41%), LaProb's call-graph label propagation (Top-1 14-68% across projects), Gharibi et al.'s stack-trace+keyword+historical Naive Bayes hybrid (Top-1≈52%, MAP 0.32-0.65). All of this operates at file/subfolder granularity — none of it reaches into method/line territory, which corroborates that the gap roust sits in (region/line, not file) is genuinely under-served by the field's strongest non-neural baselines, not just by roust's specific implementation.

**Bug-inducing-commit identification via graph search (not directly transplantable).** "Beyond Blame: Rethinking SZZ with Knowledge Graph Search" / AgenticSZZ (arXiv:2602.02934, 2026) builds a temporal knowledge graph (commits/files/functions with PRECEDES/MODIFIES/DEFINED_IN edges) and has an LLM agent traverse it to find bug-inducing commits SZZ/git-blame miss (>40% of cases), gaining 6.6-26.6% F1 over LLM4SZZ. Mechanism is graph-structured and could in principle be walked deterministically, but the paper's implementation is LLM-agent-driven — flag as a research bet, not a validated deterministic recipe, and it targets commit identification (which change broke it) rather than edit-location prediction (where will the next edit be), a related but distinct problem.

**Program slicing (static, execution-free, but not measured recently at this granularity).** Static PDG/def-use slicing from a query-named anchor symbol remains, as in wave1, execution-free and in principle usable to restrict candidate lines to a data/control-dependence cone from a matched identifier — but I found no 2023-2026 paper measuring this specifically as a *ranking* signal (as opposed to a binary include/exclude filter) at method/line granularity; "SliceT5" (arXiv:2509.17338) and related 2024-2025 work push slicing toward *learned* prediction of slices, moving away from the deterministic, training-free requirement here.

## Caveats

Linespots (candidate #2) is a 2020/2021 paper, outside the nominal 2023-2026 window — included because no 2023-2026 successor was found that improves on its specific line-level recency-decay recipe, and because it is the most rigorously measured, directly reimplementable instance of the exact signal the question asks for (mirrors wave1's inclusion of pre-2023 foundational items like the ISSTA'17 PageRank paper). Several PDFs (SBEST, ABB EASE-2026, GLANCE original TOSEM via ACM DL) initially returned only compressed binary streams through WebFetch and had to be re-extracted via direct PDF page reads; GLANCE-LR's exact formula and GLANCE++'s full numeric tables were confirmed this way and are load-bearing above, but the original 2023 GLANCE TOSEM paper's own top-line Recall@20%LOC/MCC numbers are reported here only via the 2025 follow-up's replication table (Table 3), not the original PDF directly — worth a spot-check against the ACM version before treating as final. DepGraph (FSE 2024) and its exact method-level numbers were re-confirmed as already covered in wave1 and deliberately not repeated in depth here.
